# Procedural Storytelling

## Definition

Procedural storytelling is the use of algorithmic systems to generate narrative content in interactive media. Unlike purely mechanical procedural generation (which produces assets, levels, or textures), procedural storytelling focuses on generating coherent narrative experiences — characters with personalities, plot events with causal relationships, dialog that responds to context, and world histories with internal consistency. The term was popularized as a distinct discipline through works like "Procedural Storytelling in Game Design" (Short & Adams, 2019).

## Key Properties

- Spans a spectrum from fully authored to fully procedural, with most successful implementations blending both approaches
- Requires narrative coherence — events must have causal or thematic relationships, not just random variety
- Often relies on hybrid architectures combining rule-based systems, planning algorithms, and language models
- Authoring tools and designer control are critical for practical use in game development

## Examples

- **Dwarf Fortress** (Tarn Adams) — emergent narratives from deep simulation of individual characters, history, and physics
- **The Sims** — dramatic play patterns emerging from character needs and relationships
- **Caves of Qud** — procedurally generated world histories and faction relationships
- **Frostpunk** — narrative events adapted to player choices and city state
- **Ink** (inkle) — Weave Structure for managing branching narrative complexity
- **Duskers** — curated narrative moments within a procedurally generated derelict ship environment
