# Procedural Storytelling in Dungeons & Dragons

## Definition

Steven Lumpkin examines tabletop RPG frameworks — particularly *Dungeon World*'s Moves, Dangers, and failing forward mechanics — as procedural content generation systems driven by human pattern recognition rather than algorithms. The rules act as a procedural grammar that the GM and players interpret together.

## Key Properties

- **Moves** — Narrative-mechanical categories that define what characters can do and what happens when they act
- **Dangers** — Structured threat templates: type → identity → impulse → grim portents → impending doom
- **Failing forward** — Failure does not halt progress; it introduces complications and new narrative directions
- **Moves snowball** — Small threats escalate through the move system, creating rising tension naturally
- **Soft vs Hard Moves** — Soft moves signal danger (giving players a chance to react), hard moves deliver consequences directly
- **West Marches / hexcrawl** — Open-world structures that procedurally generate adventure through player-driven exploration

## Examples

Lumpkin describes how a *Dungeon World* session produces narrative: the GM describes a situation, the player chooses a Move, the dice determine success/failure, and the GM applies consequences — creating an emergent story through system interaction.
