# Dramatic Play

## Definition

Dramatic play, introduced by Daniel Kline (Chapter 10), is a framework for understanding what makes role-playing in digital games work. Built on four fundamentals derived from *The Sims* and tabletop RPGs, it describes the conditions under which players will invest emotionally in a role and generate compelling stories through play.

## Key Properties

- **Four fundamentals**: (1) take on a role, (2) make it personal, (3) perform it, (4) the game recognizes it — all four must be present for dramatic play to emerge
- **Fail forward**: failures create new world states rather than dead ends, keeping the dramatic situation fluid
- **Dramatic currency**: rewards and progression are attached to the role being performed, not just to mechanical objectives
- **Anti-patterns**: cut-scenes that remove player agency, difficult controls that interrupt performance, fixed character personalities that prevent personalisation
- **Go broad (inelegant but rich)**: dramatic systems benefit from breadth and messiness over tight mechanical elegance — more levers create more dramatic situations

## Examples

- **The Sims** — players adopt a Sim, customise everything, perform their daily life, and the game responds with needs, relationships, and emergent events
- **Dungeons & Dragons** — the original dramatic play system where all four fundamentals are explicitly supported by the rules
- **Dwarf Fortress** — adventurer mode where the game recognises player-defined goals through its simulation systems
