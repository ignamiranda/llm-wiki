# Plot vs. Story Distinction

## Definition

Adam Saltsman argues that games are fundamentally plot generators — they produce sequences of actions and events ("subject verbs noun") — while players are story generators who construct narrative, assign motivations, and infer causality from those plots. The game's job is to produce good raw material for the player's story-construction process.

## Key Properties

- **Plot = actions/events** — The game produces sequences of "subject verbs noun" that form the raw material
- **Story = player-constructed** — Players sequence, add prose, assign motivation, and construct causality from plot fragments
- **Froth** — The storytelling that happens *after* play, when players recount what happened; this is the real story
- **Micro-stories / couplets** — Small two-part narrative units (setup + payoff) that the game can reliably produce
- **Scaffolding** — The game's systems provide structure that supports player story construction

## Examples

Saltsman uses the example of a player describing a game session: the game produced the plot (you shot the guard, alarms went off, you escaped), but the player constructs the story ("I panicked and shot too early, then had to improvise"). The froth — telling friends about it later — is where the real story emerges.
