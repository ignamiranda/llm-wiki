# Designing for a Sense of Mystery and Wonder

## Summary
Game design analysis comparing Zelda: A Link to the Past vs Breath of the Wild, extracting four strategies for designing mystery: many-to-many relationships, ambiguous space classification, overlapping gameplay modes, and inside/outside integration.

## Content

The article identifies four concrete design strategies that create a sense of mystery in games. First, many-to-many relationships between curiosity and outcome: multiple ways to satisfy the same curiosity, and the same outcome satisfying multiple curiosities. This creates a richer web of discovery than one-to-one mappings, where the player immediately knows what every lever does.

Second, ambiguous space classification: spaces that don't clearly announce their importance. In Breath of the Wild, the world avoids obvious signposting — a cave might hide a shrine or lead to a dead end. This ambiguity keeps the player uncertain and engaged, contrasting with A Link to the Past's clearer dungeon-entrance markers.

Third, overlapping gameplay modes: different game mechanics interleaved in the same space. Rather than having discrete "puzzle rooms" and "combat rooms," mixing modes within a single space prevents predictability and keeps the player alert. Fourth, integrating inside and outside spaces: blurring the boundary between interior and exterior environments, so the player never quite knows where they are or what rules apply.

The analysis contrasts A Link to the Past's carefully structured dungeon design (which the author also values) with Breath of the Wild's more open-ended approach, arguing that both have merit but cultivate different kinds of mystery. The article was later referenced by Mark Brown in his Game Maker's Toolkit video series.

## Key Takeaways
- Many-to-many relationships between curiosity and outcome create richer mystery
- Ambiguous spaces encourage exploration without clear markers
- Overlapping gameplay modes prevent predictability
- Integration of inside/outside spaces blurs expectations
