# Fail Forward

## Definition

Fail forward is a design principle from tabletop role-playing (adapted to digital games across multiple chapters of the book) where player failure does not halt progress but instead advances the story in unexpected directions. A failed lockpick check breaks the tools and alerts guards; a failed persuasion attempt offends the NPC and creates a new enemy. The story never stalls — it pivots.

## Key Properties

- **Failures trigger new narrative states**: a failed action changes the world rather than producing a null result
- **No reload required**: players should never need to save-scum because failure is as interesting as success
- **Escalates tension**: modelled after D&D GM Moves where failures introduce complications, expend resources, or reveal threats
- **At least 20% of choices should fail forward**: Daniel Kline's heuristic — a significant minority of player decisions should produce interesting failure outcomes

## Examples

- **The Sims** — a failed romantic interaction creates embarrassment and relationship damage, generating new dramatic situations
- **Dungeons & Dragons** — GM Moves like "separate the party" or "announce future badness" on failed player rolls
- **Frostpunk** — failing to meet a resource deadline escalates discontent rather than ending the game
