# Tarot as Procedural Storytelling

## Definition

Cat Manning, in Chapter 26, presents tarot as an analogue procedural storytelling system that has been generating meaningful narratives for centuries. Unlike digital generators that struggle with the oatmeal problem, tarot achieves perceptual diversity through multiple overlapping taxonomies of meaning — suits, numbers, Major/Minor Arcana, positional significance, and upright/reversed orientations — that require active interpretive construction by the reader.

## Key Properties

- **Overlapping taxonomies**: suits, numbers, Major/Minor Arcana, spread positions, and upright/reversed orientation — each card carries meaning in multiple independent dimensions simultaneously
- **4.675 × 10²¹ possible 10-card spreads**: combinatorial explosion that dwarfs digital generators, achieved with only 78 physical cards
- **Spread types**: different spatial arrangements encode different narrative structures — 1-card (focus), 3-card (past-present-future), Celtic Cross (complex situation analysis)
- **Reader-querent back-and-forth**: meaning is co-constructed through dialogue, not decoded from a fixed lookup table — the reader's interpretive framing is part of the system

## Examples

- **Celtic Cross spread** — 10 positions each modifying the card's meaning (self, challenge, past, future, hopes, outcome, etc.), producing narratives that feel personally relevant despite being procedurally generated
- **1-card daily draw** — minimal procedural generation that invites maximum interpretive engagement
