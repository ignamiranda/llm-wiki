# Level Design Workflows

## Summary
Three complementary document types for overcoming level design block: progression text docs, free-hand world map sketches, and dependency diagrams inspired by Mark Brown's Boss Keys.

## Content

When faced with level design writer's block, the solution is to work with three complementary document types, each providing a different lens on the same design problem. The first is a progression text document: a step-by-step description of what the player does, in order. This keeps the design grounded in player experience rather than abstract systems.

The second type is free-hand world map sketches on paper. The deliberate choice of paper over digital tools prevents over-investment in a specific layout — a hand-drawn sketch is clearly provisional, while a polished tilemap feels finished and resists change. Paper sketches encourage rapid iteration and exploration of alternative layouts.

The third type is a dependency diagram inspired by Mark Brown's Boss Keys series. These diagrams use a graph notation to show lock-and-key relationships between areas: which items open which doors, which areas can only be reached after obtaining certain abilities. Seeing the dependency structure in graph form clarifies whether the progression is linear, branching, or web-like, and reveals potential softlocks or bottlenecks that would be hard to spot in a map or text description.

## Key Takeaways
- Three document types: text progression, hand-drawn maps, dependency diagrams
- Hand-drawn sketches avoid over-investment in specific layouts
- Boss Keys-style dependency graphs clarify lock-and-key progression
